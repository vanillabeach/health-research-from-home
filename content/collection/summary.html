Health Research from Home: creating a new UK environment to advance population health research using smartphones and wearables

Will Dixon, Soren Brage, Richard Dobson, Lynn Laidlaw, Jenni Quint,
Charlotte Sharp, Clare Steves, Cathie Sudlow, P Mihaela van der Schaar, Sabine van der Veer


100,000 views

Much of our understanding of the distribution, causes and consequences of disease comes from epidemiology or population health research. This field of science has evolved over decades, moving from the ‘shoe leather epidemiology’ of John Snow during the 1854 Cholera epidemic, to population surveys, to cohort studies and registers, and to ‘real world data’ using routinely collected health information from administrative data and electronic health records. Much of this real-world data is collected by researchers or clinicians, typically at one-off assessments or at irregularly spaced visits.

The widespread uptake of consumer technology by patients and the public provides a new platform to advance our understanding of the causes and consequences of disease, creating the opportunity for people to contribute to health research by providing data from home. Such electronic person- generated health data (ePGHD) collected via smartphones and wearables will allow top priority, as- yet unanswered research questions to be answered, such as the examples from James Lind Alliance Priority Setting Partnerships (1) shown in Box 1. Specific benefits that unlock these opportunities include the convenience and ease of participation; the ability to share richer information about their disease – for example, tracking how their physical and mental health symptoms change day-to-day, providing objective data about their environment, their behaviour (such as physical activity) and performance in digital physical and mental tasks; lower costs by avoiding the need for research visit appointments; plus the opportunity to develop a more direct, two-way relationship with research participants, allowing research results to be fed back directly (ref- Charting a Course).

The aim of this Partnership Grant is to bring together, strengthen and advance the interdisciplinary community necessary for success in conducting health research from home. We will do this by:

  1. collating best practice in smartphone and wearable population health research across the assembled partnership and spreading this to the wider community (WP1 and 2)
  2. pioneering the new era of using smartphone and wearable data linked to existing research databanks (WP3), using the assembled people, methods and technology across the new partnership.
Ultimately, this will allow research questions to be addressed that have been difficult to answer to date (Box 1), and will position the UK as a global leader in innovative, participatory population health research.
